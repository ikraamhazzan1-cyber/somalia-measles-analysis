1. Background & Objectives

Measles remains one of the most contagious infectious diseases known, with a basic reproduction number (R₀) of 12–18. Achieving and sustaining population immunity requires MCV1 coverage of at least 95% — a threshold no Somali region has reached as of 2023. Somalia's protracted humanitarian crisis, characterized by chronic conflict, widespread internal displacement, and fragile health infrastructure, has created persistent immunization gaps that place millions of children at risk of preventable measles outbreaks.

Critical Context With 125,025 unvaccinated children identified across Somalia in 2023 alone, and four regions classified as critical (<60% MCV1), the conditions for large-scale measles outbreaks are present. This analysis quantifies those gaps and identifies their structural drivers to inform targeted intervention.

2. Methods

Study Design

A retrospective cross-sectional analysis was conducted using district-level immunization surveillance data from seven Somali regions (Banadir, Southwest, Hirshabelle, Jubaland, Puntland, Galmudug, and Somaliland) over five years (2019–2023). Data structure reflects WHO/UNICEF Joint Reporting Form (JRF) immunization indicators.

Outcomes & Variables

Primary outcomes were MCV1 and MCV2 coverage (%). Secondary outcomes included number of zero-dose (unvaccinated) children and immunization gap (100 − MCV1%). Structural determinants examined included distance to nearest health facility (km), IDP population percentage, conflict intensity score (0–7), and health worker density.

Statistical Analysis

Pearson correlation coefficients assessed associations between structural determinants and MCV1 coverage. Descriptive statistics characterized regional and temporal distribution. Geographic visualization used coordinate-based bubble mapping to represent spatial distribution of coverage and unvaccinated burden. All analyses were conducted in Python 3.12.

3. Results

3.1 Regional Coverage vs WHO Target

No Somali region achieved the WHO MCV1 target of 95% during the study period. In 2023, coverage ranged from 46% in Southwest State to 83% in Somaliland — a 37 percentage point gap between the lowest and highest performing regions. Four regions (Southwest, Galmudug, Jubaland, and Hirshabelle) remain in critical status with MCV1 below 60%. The MCV2 dropout rate — the gap between first and second dose — was substantial across all regions, indicating systemic failures in follow-up vaccination.

3.2 Trends Over Time

All regions demonstrated modest upward trends in MCV1 coverage between 2019 and 2023, suggesting incremental programmatic improvement. However, the rate of increase (approximately 1–2 percentage points per year) is insufficient to reach the WHO 95% target within a decade at current pace. The MCV2 coverage gap persisted throughout the study period, indicating structural barriers to second-dose delivery beyond the reach of first-dose campaigns.

3.3 Unvaccinated Children

The heatmap reveals that Southwest State consistently accumulates the largest absolute burden of unvaccinated children, driven by a combination of large target population and critically low coverage. The total unvaccinated burden across Somalia increased from approximately 118,000 in 2019 to 125,025 in 2023 — reflecting population growth outpacing vaccination program expansion.

Figure 3. Heatmap of unvaccinated (zero-dose) children by region and year. Southwest consistently records the highest burden, representing the primary geographic priority for targeted outreach.

3.4 Structural Determinants

Three structural factors showed strong negative correlations with MCV1 coverage. IDP population percentage showed the strongest inverse association (r = −0.939, p<0.001), followed by conflict intensity score (r = −0.928) and distance to clinic (r = −0.876). These findings confirm that humanitarian displacement, insecurity, and geographic access barriers are the primary drivers of immunization failure in Somalia — not vaccine hesitancy or supply shortfalls alone.

Figure 4. Scatter plots of MCV1 coverage against distance to clinic (left), IDP population percentage (centre), and conflict intensity score (right). All three show strong negative correlations, confirming structural drivers of coverage failure.

3.5 Geographic Distribution

The geographic bubble map reveals a clear spatial pattern: northern regions (Somaliland, Puntland) achieve higher coverage, while southern and central regions (Southwest, Hirshabelle, Jubaland, Galmudug) remain in critical status. Bubble size represents unvaccinated child burden — Southwest and Hirshabelle emerge as the highest-priority geographic targets for intensified immunization intervention.

Figure 5. Geographic distribution of MCV1 coverage across Somalia in 2023. Bubble size = number of unvaccinated children. Color = coverage classification. A clear north–south gradient is visible.

4. Key Findings

Finding 01

Zero regions in Somalia achieved the WHO MCV1 target of 95% — creating a persistent measles outbreak risk across the entire country.

Finding 02

IDP displacement is the strongest predictor of low coverage (r=−0.939), requiring humanitarian-integrated immunization delivery models.

Finding 03

125,025 children remained unvaccinated in 2023 — with Southwest State and Hirshabelle accounting for over 40% of national burden.

Finding 04

The north–south gradient in coverage reflects the security and infrastructure disparity — Somaliland's relative stability translates directly to better immunization outcomes.

5. Recommendations

Priority 1 — Southwest & Hirshabelle Emergency ResponseWith MCV1 below 50% and over 51,000 unvaccinated children combined, these two regions require immediate supplementary immunization activities (SIAs) targeting zero-dose children in IDP settlements.
Priority 2 — IDP-Integrated OutreachGiven the r=−0.939 correlation between IDP density and low coverage, fixed-facility vaccination is insufficient. Mobile vaccination teams embedded within IDP camp management structures are required for these populations.
Priority 3 — MCV2 Dropout ReductionThe consistent MCV1–MCV2 gap across all regions indicates second-dose delivery fails systematically. Defaulter tracing, reminder systems, and community health worker follow-up should be strengthened in all regions.
Priority 4 — Somaliland as ModelSomaliland's relatively higher coverage (83% MCV1) in a Somali context demonstrates that improved security and community health infrastructure yield measurable immunization gains. This model should inform investment in other regions as conditions permit.

6. Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates that measles vaccination coverage in Somalia remains critically below WHO thresholds across all seven regions, with 125,025 children unvaccinated in 2023 alone. The strong associations between coverage failure and IDP displacement, conflict, and geographic access barriers confirm that immunization gaps in Somalia are fundamentally humanitarian problems requiring humanitarian solutions. Targeted investment in mobile outreach, IDP-integrated service delivery, and second-dose follow-up in Southwest State and Hirshabelle represents the highest-yield opportunity to reduce measles outbreak risk in the near term.
